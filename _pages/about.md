I am a Ph.D. Candidate in Agricultural and Applied Economics at the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign and a Senior Research Associate at the National Center for Supercomputing Applications (NCSA).

I study how **connectedness between people and places shapes individuals' choices and socioeconomic outcomes**, particularly under conditions of economic and environmental stress such as climate change, conflict, and displacement. Much of my work centers on the **labor decisions of households**, from migration to the intrahousehold division of labor, spanning gender and labor norms, forced displacement, and food security across South Asia, the Middle East, and Latin America. I combine machine learning, geospatial methods, and causal inference to study these questions.

Before my Ph.D., I spent over five years in the international development sector across South Asia, directing grassroots programs, founding a women's collective-entrepreneurship venture, and advising organizations including the World Bank, the ILO, and UNDP. That experience continues to shape the questions I ask and the communities my research centers.

## Job Market Paper

***Uncovering the Influence of Hidden Social Networks on Gendered Labor Norms: Evidence from Rural India***

Economics has traditionally characterized spousal labor sharing as the outcome of an intrahousehold bargaining process; however, these decisions are also shaped by norms embedded within and transmitted through social networks. This paper investigates the influence of peer behavior on labor sharing between spouses. A key empirical challenge is that social network ties are unobserved in most existing datasets. I overcome this by jointly estimating the latent social structure, peer effects, and contextual effects within a linear social interactions model via Adaptive Elastic Net Generalized Method of Moments, recovering network architecture without requiring directly observed network data. Using the *Village Dynamics in South Asia* (VDSA) dataset across 16 villages and approximately 629 households, I find that within-sample peer networks governing production and domestic labor-sharing differ in topology. Domestic norms exhibit a moderate social multiplier: a 10-percentage-point increase in peers' male-to-female domestic work ratio is associated with a 5.8-percentage-point increase in a couple's own ratio. The corresponding production peer effect is 5.4 percentage points. Exploratory analysis of the recovered networks shows that couples maintain influence ties with roughly three peers on average, and that this degree is stable across village sizes; as a result, estimated network density declines as villages grow larger, a mechanical consequence of a roughly fixed per-couple relationship budget spread over a larger pool of potential partners. I also examine assortativity, the tendency to connect with similar peers. Production-norm networks are positively caste- and land-assortative, meaning same-caste and similarly-landed couples are tied more often than chance would predict; domestic-norm networks show no such assortativity on either dimension. Influence is likewise domain-specific: non-Hindu households have lower influence over production norms, while forward-caste households have somewhat higher influence over domestic norms, with a marginally significant effect. My findings provide novel insights and evidence on how network-mediated spillover effects are integral in shaping intrahousehold labor distributions.
