I am a Ph.D. Candidate in Agricultural and Applied Economics at the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign and a Senior Research Associate at the National Center for Supercomputing Applications (NCSA).

I study how **connectedness between people and places shapes individuals' choices and socioeconomic outcomes**, particularly under conditions of economic and environmental stress such as climate change, conflict, and displacement. Much of my work centers on the **labor decisions of households**, from migration to the intrahousehold division of labor, spanning gender and labor norms, forced displacement, and food security across South Asia, the Middle East, and Latin America. I combine machine learning, geospatial methods, and causal inference to study these questions.

Before my Ph.D., I spent over five years in the international development sector across South Asia, directing grassroots programs, founding a women's collective-entrepreneurship venture, and advising organizations including the World Bank, the ILO, and UNDP. That experience continues to shape the questions I ask and the communities my research centers.

## Job Market Paper

***Uncovering the Influence of Hidden Social Networks on Gendered Labor Norms: Evidence from Rural India***

Economics has traditionally characterized spousal labor sharing as the outcome of an intrahousehold bargaining process; however, these decisions are also shaped by norms embedded within and transmitted through social networks. This paper investigates the influence of peer behavior on labor sharing between spouses. To do this, I jointly estimate latent social structures, peer effects, and contextual effects via a linear social interactions model, employing an Adaptive Elastic Net Generalized Method of Moments approach to recover the structure of the network using microdata for rural Indian households. I find that the peer networks governing household production and domestic labor sharing differ in both their topology and the size of the spillover effects that they generate. Domestic labor sharing exhibits a strong social multiplier: peers' male-to-female domestic labor ratio passes through to a couple's own ratio at 0.58, while the passthrough for the female-to-male production ratio is more modest, at 0.54. More caste-diverse villages form denser production-norm networks, and densely connected sub-clusters within networks do not mirror caste or religious divisions. Women who have ever belonged to a self-help group (SHG) are central influencers in production-norm networks. These findings provide novel insights on how network-mediated spillover effects shape intrahousehold labor distributions, and more broadly, on how peer effects operate through the social networks in which households are embedded.
