**Abstract.** The global digital transformation of financial services has heightened concerns about gender disparities in financial inclusion and empowerment, particularly as gender gaps in financial literacy often emerge early in life. This study leverages data from the 2018 PISA assessment to examine gender differences in the relationship between financial literacy and both traditional and digital financial inclusion among youth. Using the Lewbel method to address endogeneity, the analysis reveals that financial literacy significantly enhances financial and digital financial inclusion. However, persistent gender disparities remain, especially in traditional financial inclusion, where girls are less likely than boys to own bank accounts or payment cards. Gaps in confidence are also notable, with girls up to 50 percentage points less likely to feel confident performing financial and digital tasks compared to boys. Encouragingly, participation in a financial literacy course significantly improves girls' confidence, highlighting its potential to reduce these disparities. Yet greater confidence does not necessarily translate to increased financial service participation, suggesting the continued presence of external barriers to financial inclusion. These findings underscore the need for targeted policy interventions to promote early financial education, digital skill development, and confidence-building among girls, laying a foundation for their economic empowerment in the digital age.
