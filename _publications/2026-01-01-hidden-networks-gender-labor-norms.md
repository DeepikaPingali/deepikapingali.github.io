**Job Market Paper.** Economics has traditionally treated how spouses divide labor as the outcome of intrahousehold bargaining, but these decisions are also shaped by norms transmitted through social networks. In this paper I study peer influence on spousal labor sharing in rural India, jointly estimating the latent network structure, peer effects, and contextual effects with a linear social-interactions model, using an Adaptive Elastic Net GMM approach to recover the network from household microdata. I find a strong social multiplier: peers' domestic labor-sharing ratio passes through to a couple's own at 0.58, and I show that the networks governing domestic versus productive labor differ in both topology and spillover size, with women who have belonged to self-help groups emerging as central influencers. These findings show that network-mediated spillovers are integral to how households allocate labor, with implications for how we model peer effects and intrahousehold decisions more broadly.
