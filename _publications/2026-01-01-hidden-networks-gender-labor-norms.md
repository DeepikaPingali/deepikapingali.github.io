**Job Market Paper.** Economics has traditionally characterized spousal labor sharing as the outcome of an intrahousehold bargaining process; however, these decisions are also shaped by norms embedded within and transmitted through social networks. This paper investigates the influence of peer behavior on labor sharing between spouses. To do this, I jointly estimate latent social structures, peer effects, and contextual effects via a linear social interactions model, employing an Adaptive Elastic Net Generalized Method of Moments approach to recover the structure of the network using microdata for rural Indian households. I find that the peer networks governing household production and domestic labor sharing differ in both their topology and the size of the spillover effects that they generate. Domestic labor sharing exhibits a strong social multiplier: peers' male-to-female domestic labor ratio passes through to a couple's own ratio at 0.58, while the passthrough for the female-to-male production ratio is more modest, at 0.54. More caste-diverse villages form denser production-norm networks, and densely connected sub-clusters within networks do not mirror caste or religious divisions. Women who have ever belonged to a self-help group (SHG) are central influencers in production-norm networks. These findings provide novel insights on how network-mediated spillover effects shape intrahousehold labor distributions, and more broadly, on how peer effects operate through the social networks in which households are embedded.
